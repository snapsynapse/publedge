## Summary

The IRS Office of Chief Counsel ruled that an incentive-fee management contract between a §501(c)(3) bond issuer and a hotel manager does not cause private business use under IRC §141, despite the incentive fee being calculated on a metric derived in part from net profit variables. The ruling applies the Rev. Proc. 2017-13 safe harbor and concludes that, on the represented facts, the contract's compensation arrangement is not a prohibited "share of net profits" within the meaning of the safe harbor.

## Background

Tax-exempt bond proceeds fund facilities owned by §501(c)(3) organizations. Private business use of those facilities can jeopardize the tax-exempt status of the bonds under IRC §141. Rev. Proc. 2017-13 provides a safe harbor for service-provider arrangements, notably permitting compensation not based on a share of net profits of the financed facility.

The requesting taxpayer (identity redacted) had entered a long-term hotel management agreement where the manager received a base fee plus an incentive fee calculated as a percentage of gross revenues, subject to an adjustment contingent on two operational metrics, one of which the IRS described as a "variant of net profits." The taxpayer sought a ruling that the structure remained within the safe harbor.

## Question presented

Whether a management contract whose incentive-fee component is contingent on a metric derived in part from net-profit-variant inputs constitutes a "share of net profits" impermissible under Rev. Proc. 2017-13 §5.02(2), and therefore results in private business use under §141.

## Holding

On the specific facts represented, the management contract does not constitute sharing of net profits within the meaning of Rev. Proc. 2017-13, and accordingly does not result in private business use under §141. The bonds remain eligible for tax-exempt treatment.

## Limitations

- Binding only on the requesting taxpayer and only on the represented facts. Material factual variance voids the ruling.
- Redaction removes facts that would typically be load-bearing for a similarly-situated third party trying to assess whether their arrangement parallels the ruling.
- Does not address state or local tax treatment of the bonds or the management contract.
- Rev. Proc. 2017-13 may itself be modified or superseded; such change would not retroactively alter this ruling but would affect any party contemplating a similar structure.
